One bed does the work of twenty.

20 to 30×

One tomato bed versus one bed of beets, kale, or onions.

Tomatoes already sit on most market gardens, and their ceiling is higher than any other crop you're growing. A well-run bed of greenhouse indeterminate tomatoes earns what 20 to 30 beds of beets, kale, or onions earn.

That's the lever.

When yield doubles and your market already buys every tomato you grow, you can cut half the beds and keep the sales, or keep the beds and automate the work, so the greenhouse stops demanding your attention around the clock.

Does this fit your farm, and your week?

This is built for you if

  • •You're a market gardener a few years in.
  • •You already grow greenhouse indeterminate tomatoes.
  • •You've been through at least one tomato season.
  • •You're open to trying things on the plant you haven't tried.

It probably isn't for you if

  • •You're not growing greenhouse tomatoes yet.
  • •You run an industrial-scale greenhouse.
  • •You're looking for academic theory, not hands-on practice.

We'd rather tell you up front than have you waste a season on the wrong thing.

What a month looks like

On the 1st, a five-minute video lands in your inbox with the single goal for the month. First month: building fruit load without exhausting the plants.

You watch it between chores. Later in the month, Guillaume and Andrew run a live call, about 90 minutes, where you bring photos and questions from your own tunnel.

Between those, you work your own plants. Not a case study, not homework. The plants that were already on your plan this week, with a sharper idea of what to look for.

At the end of the month, there's a new goal. And the plant is further ahead than it would have been.
