# Gordon raw notes, 2025 season

Unedited field notes from Gordon at Ten Mothers Farm through the 2025 greenhouse tomato season. Preserved verbatim for story context and future reference.

Gordon joined remote consulting with Orisha in late spring 2025. Within a week he wrote back that the pollination-by-tapping protocol had doubled fruit load in 2 weeks. Heat and bacterial spot then erased the gains. Retrospective lessons added to the playbook: prune fruit to preserve plant vigor for fall productivity, and select heat-resistant varieties from the start.

(2) Plants went in a week later than usual (early March) after experiencing cold damage during a power outage in January.

(3) We're seeing some suckers sprouting from flower clusters.

(1) It's been cool and cloudy all week.

(2) My crew noticed that the stems are thicker already.

(3) Pollination is improving: I can see that flowers are setting fruit earlier now that I'm shaking the plants every day.

(4) A few leaves showing what I believe is potassium deficiency (see photo). We're currently fertigating with 40# K/acre.

(1) Unusually cloudy and cool; just started to warm up yesterday.

(2) Two days ago, I finally dialed in the irrigation enough that there was no guttation.

(3) More flower clusters are horizontal, instead of vertical.

---

(1) Stem thickness is only a little on the skinny side, though it varies a lot by variety. (Which shows that we could benefit from grafted plants.)

(2) I'm not seeing widespread blossom drop right now, despite the heat.

(3) I'm averaging the week's harvest across all the leaders in the tunnel (480 in total). So, in the last 7 days we've harvested 258 lbs.

---

(1) On some plants, I'm noticing the upper leaves turning a lighter shade of green, and signs of potassium deficiency on younger leaves has become a bit more widespread (despite my heavy applications).

(2) Stem thickness and fruit set varies significantly by variety. Some varieties still look decently vigorous and are setting fruit normally; others are very skinny and have a lot of blossoms dropping or not getting pollinated.

(3) Daytime temperatures in the tunnel are in the high 90s (F) every day (and will be well above 100 next week), and nighttime temperatures outdoors are at 70 F or higher. This is early for it to be that hot, but not unheard of.

---

(1) The tunnel has now been shaded (30%) for a week, and there's been no change in stem thickness. Honestly, some (not many) stems are a little thicker.

(2) Pollination has improved in the last week, though it's still not perfect. I'm attaching photos from two clusters on the same plant: the cluster from two weeks ago hasn't set any fruit, but the cluster from last week has 2 fruit so far.

(3) Leaf color has improved slightly as well. Some upper leaves had been turning a paler shade of green; now there are fewer looking pale. That could be from our weekly fertigation, too.

(4) Harvest yield was down a bit this week, but I think that's because we're getting into the clusters that set very few fruit during our aphid invasion.

---

(1) Stems may have thickened, just slightly. They're definitely not skinnier.

(2) 3 varieties are still setting fruit during the heat wave: Geronimo, Cuba Libre, Enroza. One variety is not: Margold. It also has very skinny stems. I'm tapping the plants nearly every day to increase pollination. I'm including photos of a Geronimo plant and a Margold plant, for reference.

(3) I've switched to logging data on Mondays, so the interval from last week was only 5 days, not 7. (Which means 2 harvests, not 3 as usual. We harvest M-W-F.)

---

(1) None of the varieties set much fruit last week, including Geronimo. We have other varieties in other tunnels (including new plantings, 4 weeks old) and none of those set much fruit, either. Both shaded and unshaded tunnels: bad fruit set on slicing tomatoes. Cherry tomatoes are still setting fruit.

(2) Stems continue to thicken, one variety, Cuba Libre, is now at AA battery. In general, in this tunnel, all the plants are showing a little more vigor.

(1) Fruit set has only very slightly improved, most clusters are setting 0-2 fruit. Enroza is currently setting fruit better than Geronimo.

(2) Geronimo is planted on the edge beds, which probably absorbed more water than usual (we got 15 inches of rain in the last week). The sides were down, but I'm sure water seeps in. Some early blight showed up on the lower leaves of one of the Geronimo beds, and we pruned those leaves off.

(3) I'm attaching photos showing a vigorous stem (Cuba Libre) and poor fruit set (Geronimo)

---

(1) Fruit set is improving a little more, but still isn't great.

(2) We have bacterial spot on green fruit in the Geronimo plants (see photo), which are in the edge beds of this tunnel. We also have it bad on peppers in the field. So far, it's been the rainiest and most humid summer on record, so the disease isn't a big surprise.

(3) I thought I'd share a photo of what some clusters look like, where the flowers are never even opening. This is particularly true on the Margold plants, but happens occasionally in other varieties too.

---

(1) Bacterial spot has spread. Geronimo has it the worst; Cuba Libre is fending it off best. We leaf-pruned hard and are spraying Serenade.

(2) Even though the plants must be stressed, the top 2-3 feet of the plants generally looks good: AA battery, healthy color, still putting out 3 leaves and 1 cluster every week. Fruit set is still inconsistent.

(3) We may be close to the point where I don't think it's worth it to continue this level of maintenance, and we'll just let the plants go and try to harvest last ripe fruit in early September.
