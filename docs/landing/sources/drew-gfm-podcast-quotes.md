## Headline yield numbers (load-bearing for landing)

**[251] "We increased production by like 2.5 just year over year."**

**[1163] "Our slicer tomatoes now produce 350% more than they did compared to 2023. And we're up to almost five pounds per square foot. And we're trying to hit six pounds per square foot this year. We were at just around a pound per square foot with slicers in 2023. And that was still, we were one of the better tomato producers in the area."**

**[259] "We more than doubled our yields per square foot, but I'll tell you, we did not double our yields per hour of labor. There's no such thing as a free lunch. So if you want to grow more food, you've got to do more something."**

**[273] "We increased the rate that the plants were growing by 250% as well. So that nearly tripled the pruning and trellising work that we were doing weekly. In 2023, when our tomatoes weren't growing very well, we did most of our pruning and trellising on the ground and only got the ladders out in the last couple of weeks. And in 2024, we got ladders out in June."**

**[285] "We're growing more tomatoes per labor hour and more tomatoes per initial investment of putting in the beds and of the propane heating. And it specs out pretty nice on calculations."**

## Why he came to Orisha (origin / disease pressure)

**[234] "In 2023, we had had a lot of flooding in the greenhouse. And then after that, we had disease issues all summer long. Just never stopped. We had bacterial spot all summer."**

**[249] "When I heard about dehumidification of greenhouses could reduce tomato diseases, that's what really lit the light bulb. I was like, what?"**

**[361] "We tried heating without [Orisha] for a season, and that was not great."**

**[380] "[A house thermostat] makes a warm, moist environment where diseases propagate and spread fast. We had a house thermostat in our greenhouse for the first season and it caused a lot of problems."**

## Disease results after automation (the recovery story)

**[491] "We had one variety of tomato that still had some bacterial spot in 2024, but none of the other varieties did and that one had less of it than the previous year. And in 2025 we didn't have any disease issues. We had the occasional botrytis on a leaf stem that had been snapped off and there was too much leftover material. But we actually found even when that botrytis sometimes had moved from the leaf stem into the vine, we could carve it out with a knife and the vine would dry off and heal and recover. So I don't think we lost a single vine to the botrytis even though we would occasionally have a spot of it moving in."**

## Climate control, the human can't do this

**[459] "As a human, you cannot keep the temperature window of your greenhouse as precise as an automation can. Especially, it's usually not fully sunny, and so the sun's hiding behind a cloud and the sun's coming out. I look at my graphs occasionally and I see that my sides have gone up and down hundreds of times, and I can see that my temperature has stayed between 70 and 75 this whole time. Whereas before that, I get an alert on my phone, I'm like crap, it's 95 in the greenhouse, let me go open the sides, and then I get an alert a half hour later and it's 60 in my greenhouse, let me go close the sides. And tempered plants do not like a 30 40 degree temperature swing."**

**[479] "Or even when you're at market and you get that alarm that your greenhouse is 95 degrees and you're like, I got two more hours of market. I hope I still have a crop when I get home. That's not fun."**

**[397] The system "decid[es] when to open the sides, how much wind to run the fans, doing all of that juggle to keep your plants in the optimal 10 degree window for that particular hour, for that particular month, for that particular stage of growth, and if it's sunny cloudy or partly sunny."**

## Irrigation, precision and pulsing

**[547] "We used to run just like timers and we would run it for an hour at a time. If you water for an hour at a time most of that water just goes down and away. It does not stay in the topsoil."**

**[549] "Now the system modulates the irrigation based off of the month, the number of hours of daylight there are in that month, the stage of growth of the plants. We tell it how many leaves each plant has because that affects how much water they're uptaking, and then if it's sunny or not or partly cloudy. So you open up the system and it's like, it watered 11 times today for one minute and 13 seconds. So precision watering, we have almost no fruit crack issues, the fruits hardly ever splitting, and once the plants are established we almost never have signs of drought stress or over watering."**

**[605] "If you're doing it with a timer and you need to remember to go out every day and change the timing frequency based off of the weather prediction for the day, you're just not going to do it every day. And any time that your irrigation protocol doesn't match up to the plant's actual needs, that's loss in productivity."**

## Quality, no #2s

**[1050] "We don't have many problems with splitting. We have very few problems with blossom end rot. We get so few number twos. Almost everything that comes out of the greenhouse goes to market. We have a small pile of tomatoes but usually that's because somebody dropped it on the ground."**

**[1068] "Our tomatoes taste great. I didn't like tomatoes until I was like 20 years old and then I tasted a farm fresh tomato. I don't eat tomatoes for like seven months out of the year because they taste like watery disappointment. There's no decreasing quality for increasing the health of the plants."**

## Why heated greenhouse / why tomatoes (squarely on brand thesis)

**[291] "With our farm, our initial bottleneck was space. We just did not have enough space to grow enough food to be able to make a living. And so we really focused in on what are the most value-dense crops per square foot."**

**[823] "Tomatoes and hothouse crops were the most value-dense thing we could grow. The question is, I want to make a living farming, I want to feed my community. I couldn't do that without a heated greenhouse initially with the design parameters of my space."**

**[1024] "If you're at all space limited, like we are, we have one heated greenhouse, if you can make your tomatoes 15% more productive, it just makes sense. We're already burning the same amount of propane. So if you're going to have an environmentally controlled greenhouse and you're not adding 15% to your production, I just don't know. Strong advocate for grafting in a controlled environment greenhouse."**

**[1175] "Lots of incremental improvements, always learning, always trying to figure out how to make it just a little bit better, five percent better every year."**

**[918] "When you are at high levels of production, if you change production by five percent, that's a lot of fruit at the end of the year."**

## Mental health, burnout, balance

**[35] "On a farm, the mountain's work is never done. And so you have to just choose to walk away from the mountain for periods of time and to not think about it. Because you could spend every waking moment working on your farm and the work would still not be done."**

**[59] "It's a dangerous trap that a lot of us that are drawn to our professions through passion fall into. We're like, oh, if you love what you do, you'll never work a day in your life. And that's just a lie. If you love what you do, it's still work, and it's still stressful, and you need to balance it. And you need to do the things to take care of yourself."**

**[78] "We always need to reinforce the PSA that farmers need to take care of themselves. We are an at-risk population for mental health problems."**

## Values, why farm at all (use sparingly)

**[838] "As a small-scale grower, most of us go into this for values. We're not becoming farmers because we want to get rich. We're becoming farmers because we're trying to do good for the world. We're not happy with the industrial agricultural system. We're not happy with the poison that's put into the earth. We're trying to do good. So the question is, how can we actually measure that on our farm? It's good to have that value, and then it's good to have reality checks and metrics to be like, yes, I'm doing good."**

**[846] "A lot of farmers don't want to have a boss. We get in it for the small business reasons. I like to be my own boss. I could solve my own problems. Doing good is part of the motivation for sure."**

**[852] "I'm fine with not having a boss, but I love my employees. I'm very grateful for them. We have like seven part-time employees this year. We'll go out and 12 people hit up the lettuce harvest, and it's like, wow, we just harvested 150 pounds of lettuce in an hour. Or rock picking days, okay, we all picked rock for two hours. I used to do this for a whole week by myself. I love my employees."**
