## 2. 2024: the season that changed how we think

**Purpose.** Open the story with Drew's 2024 yield and the Allison moment. Name the thesis shift (pressure relief through yield) and the reason greenhouse tomatoes are the lever. This is where the "why this exists" earns its place.

**Draft copy.**

> ## The season that changed how we think about this
>
> In 2024, Drew at Ghost House Farm installed Orisha to run the climate and irrigation in his greenhouse. By the end of that season he had harvested around 2,000 pounds of tomatoes. Roughly 250% of his 2023 yield. Doubled.
>
> The moment that reframed everything for us came later, when Drew mentioned that his wife Allison had quit her job to work full-time on the farm. That was the real story. The yield was a means. The life behind the yield was the point.
>
> Every farmer we talk to loves what they do, and is quietly crushed by the pressure of it. Too much to do, all at once. So we stopped thinking about yield for its own sake and started thinking about pressure relief through yield. Cutting workload without cutting sales. Getting paid enough that you can actually live the life you chose.
>
> Greenhouse tomatoes turned out to be the best place to start. Highest revenue per bed, by a wide margin. One tomato bed can do the work of twenty or thirty beds of beets or peas. Fewer beds to weed, fewer beds to irrigate, fewer beds to coordinate staff around. That is the lever this program pulls.

**Notes.**
- Lifts the first three paragraphs of the approved "Where we are" draft nearly verbatim, with one small addition: the phrase "cutting workload without cutting sales" slotted inside the thesis line, per Guillaume's explicit request to keep it on the page.
- Ends on the one-bed-equals-twenty reframe from `positioning.md`. This is where the greenhouse tomato focus gets its justification without sounding like a product claim.
- No CTA here. The reader is leaning in, not being sold.

## 3. 2025: so we started consulting

**Purpose.** Show what happened when we tried to help other farmers. Drew's rough start and recovery, Gordon's partial win and the lesson, the single-week record, the "half of what is possible" honesty. This is also where the reader starts to see what the coaching actually looks like, because that is what we are describing.

**Draft copy.**

> ## So we started consulting
>
> In 2025 we started working with farmers remotely. Drew was our first client. Gordon at Ten Mothers Farm joined in late spring. The rhythm was simple: a weekly check-in, the Orisha data from the greenhouse, and a short written note back with what to try next. Every week, on the same day.
>
> Within a week of working together, Gordon wrote to us: *"Pollination has definitely improved, the tapping works. We've doubled fruit load in 2 weeks."* Then heat and bacterial spot erased the win. We hadn't yet learned that Gordon pauses sales in August, so we could have pruned fruit to carry the plants strong into fall. That lesson went into the playbook.
>
> Drew's 2025 season started rough. Wilted seedlings, a month behind in May. We fixed pollination in week two and fruit load went up four times in fourteen days. By July 1, he had beaten his entire 2023 season, before the point on the calendar when he had even started harvesting cherry tomatoes in 2023.
>
> *"I just looked over our 2023 numbers. In the next week or two we will have harvested more pounds of cherry tomatoes than we did in all of 2023. And we will have done so before we even started harvesting cherry tomatoes that year."*
> Drew, mid-July 2025.
>
> Even this season, with a 327 pound single-week record, Drew carried about half the fruit load a plant can actually support. Our best current result is half of what is possible.

**Notes.**
- Lifts the consulting paragraphs from `reframe-drafts.md` almost verbatim. One small addition up front: a single sentence describing what a week of coaching actually looked like (check-in, Orisha data, written note). That gives buyers a concrete picture of the delivery format without interrupting the story.
- Drew's pull quote is held intact; it carries the arc better than any claim we could make.
- "Half of what is possible" lands the paragraph. It is the most honest thing on the page and earns the reader's trust for what comes next.

## 4. What we learned, and what the playbook is becoming

**Purpose.** Translate the story into what a farmer joining the program would actually work on. Naming the themes (pollination, fruit abortion, variety, pruning for fall vigor) shows the content without turning it into a feature list. Ends with the first monthly goal.

**Draft copy.**

> ## What we learned
>
> The same two problems showed up on farm after farm. Pollination failure that looked like a variety issue but responded to mechanical tapping. Flower and fruit abortion driven by plant stress, variety choice, and climate swings. Both addressable. Both in the first month of the program.
>
> Over the 2025 season, the playbook started to take shape around a few throughlines: prune for vigor into fall, pick varieties that actually hold up in your heat, keep the plant's stress window narrow enough that it can carry the fruit load it sets. None of it is secret. A lot of it is timing and judgment, and judgment is what a coaching relationship can actually move.
>
> The program is built around that work. Each month has a goal. A few short videos frame the goal, the community works on it at the same time on their own farms, and a live coaching session handles the places where your farm is not like the slides. The first monthly goal, for the group that starts this spring, is straightforward: maximize fruit load without exhausting the plant.
>
> The playbook is not finished. We are writing it with this first group of farmers, carefully, because going faster would break what we are trying to build.

**Notes.**
- This is the only place the draft describes the coaching format directly. Kept brief on purpose: short videos, monthly goal, live coaching session. If Guillaume wants the "2 to 3 hours a week" estimate from FAQ Q1 surfaced here rather than buried in the FAQ, add one sentence: "The rhythm runs about two to three hours a week, most of it work you were already going to do."
- "None of it is secret. A lot of it is timing and judgment" is the honest framing that distinguishes coaching from a course. It also echoes FAQ Q7 without repeating it.
- Closes with the "not finished / going faster would break it" line, which sets up the capacity framing in the join section.

## Decisions Guillaume may want to push back on

- **No dedicated problem section.** The current page has a `#problem` block between hero and how-it-works. In Option 1, the farmer's reality (overwork, pressure, every-farmer-is-quietly-crushed) is named inside Section 2, paragraph 3, rather than staged as its own section. Trade-off: the story moves faster, but the classic "pain before solution" beat gets one paragraph, not a full section. If Guillaume wants that beat restored, easiest is to split Section 2 into "The season that changed how we think" and "Every farmer we talk to," with the thesis paragraph standing alone as the second section.
- **No dedicated testimonials section.** Drew and Gordon are woven through Sections 2 and 3 rather than staged as a "Real farmers. Real results." block. This matches the Option 1 premise (whole page is the story) but loses the current page's social-proof anchor. If Guillaume wants a small testimonials strip retained, it could sit between Section 4 and Section 5 as a single visual callout of Drew's pull quote.
- **Helper and Chief Grower are not described on the page.** `products.md` says "The sales call decides between Helper and Chief Grower." I treated that as permission to keep those two products off the landing page and route them through the email sequence and sales call. If Guillaume wants Helper / Chief Grower context on the landing page, the natural place is a short paragraph at the end of Section 6.
- **"Cutting workload without cutting sales" lives in the hero descriptor and the Section 2 thesis paragraph.** Both appearances are short. If Guillaume wants it more prominent (e.g. as a section header), the cleanest option is retitling Section 4 "Cutting workload without cutting sales" and letting the playbook content sit under it.
- **I kept the H1 and the poetic second line sacred and untouched.** I did also keep the existing hero signup form as-is. If the whole-page-is-story frame argues for delaying the form until the reader has read the story, we could remove the hero form and make the hero CTA scroll to Section 6. Open question.
- **I kept pricing in Section 6 only, not earlier.** Mentioning $40/mo during the story would break the voice. The risk: a scanner who stops reading after Section 3 never sees the price. Counter-risk: a pricing mention in the middle of the story reads as salesy, which is off-limits. I chose to protect the voice and make Section 6 unmissable via a prominent block and a navigation anchor in the top nav.
- **Top nav links to keep:** How it works, Pricing, Join. In Option 1, "How it works" would anchor to Section 4 (What we learned), "Pricing" to Section 6, "Join" to Section 6 or Section 8. If Guillaume wants a "Story" anchor in the nav too, add one pointing to Section 2.
- **The word "cohort" does not appear on the page.** The approved "Where we are" and final CTA drafts both used "Cohort 1." I swapped to "the program" or "this first group" everywhere. Flagging because `reframe-drafts.md` still uses the word; this draft is the first place it has been removed end-to-end per the brief.
