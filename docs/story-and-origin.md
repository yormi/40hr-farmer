# Story and origin

Canonical timeline and facts behind the 40hr Farmer program. Reference document for internal use, not marketing copy. Use this to verify details before writing anything customer-facing.

## 2024 season, Ghost House Farm (Drew)

- Drew installs Orisha (the climate and irrigation tool) on his greenhouse.
- End of 2024 season yield: roughly **2000 lbs of tomatoes, 250% of his 2023 yield**. Yield doubled.
- Public Instagram documentation:
  - `https://www.instagram.com/p/C6O7kEZOsdY/` — Orisha automation caption. (Labeled informally as the "2000 lbs" post, but the yield proof is actually on the year-in-review post below.)
  - `https://www.instagram.com/p/DEQhrJXORKw/` — Dec 31 2024 year-in-review. Contains the lines "Improved tomato production by 250% with new techniques and system" and "We made a profit for the first time, yay!".

## Late 2024, after the doubled yield

- Drew tells us his wife **Allison quit her job to work full-time on the farm**.
- This is the moment the positioning flipped from "yield tool" to "pressure-relief lever". See [`brand/positioning.md`](brand/positioning.md).

## 2025 season, remote consulting starts

### Drew (Ghost House Farm)

- First remote consulting client, starting late spring 2025.
- May: plants were **wilted** (not flooded, the earlier shorthand was wrong) and roughly a month behind schedule.
- Week 2: pollination fix triggered a **4x fruit load increase in 2 weeks**.
- Beat his full 2023 season yield by **July 1, 2025**, before he had even harvested cherry tomatoes in 2023.
- Single-week record: **327 lbs of slicers mid-July 2025**. Previous record was 227 lbs.
- Even with the 2025 gains, plants produced only about **half of the fruit load they could physically carry**. Our best current result is roughly half of what the plant is capable of.
- The published 15-episode season writeup lives at `drew-season/episode-01.html` through `episode-15.html`.
- Raw unpublished field notes at [`drew-raw-notes.md`](drew-raw-notes.md).
- The "more cherry tomatoes than all of 2023" line comes from the raw notes. That is the canonical "too many tomatoes" moment.

### Gordon (Ten Mothers Farm)

- Joined remote consulting spring 2025.
- Within a week of working together, Gordon wrote: *"Pollination has definitely improved, the tapping works. We've doubled fruit load in 2 weeks."*
- Heat plus bacterial spot later erased the early win.
- We later learned Gordon **pauses sales in August**, which changed how we would have approached the season.
- Retrospective lessons, now folded into the playbook:
  1. We could have pruned fruit to keep plant vigor through fall.
  2. We could have picked heat-resistant varieties earlier in planning.
- Raw unpublished field notes (weekly check-ins) at [`gordon-raw-notes.md`](gordon-raw-notes.md).

## Pattern that emerged across 2025

The same two problems showed up on farm after farm:

- **Pollination failure** that looked like variety or heat issues but responded to mechanical intervention (tapping).
- **Flower and fruit abortion** driven by plant stress, variety choice, and climate swings.

Both are addressable. Both are in the Month 1 teaching plan for Cohort 1.

## What this origin story tells us (and does not)

- It tells us greenhouse tomato yield is a real lever, not a theoretical one. Two farms have seen it move.
- It tells us the lever lands as **family and lifestyle change**, not just bigger harvests. Allison's career pivot is the signal.
- It does **not** tell us we can guarantee any specific outcome for any specific farm. We have a small sample, heat and disease can still erase wins, and our best client still only reached about half of the plant's theoretical capacity.
- Copy implication: show the stories, never promise the result. See [`brand/voice-and-values.md`](brand/voice-and-values.md).
