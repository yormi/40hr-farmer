# Drew raw notes, 2025 season

Unedited field notes from Drew at Ghost House Farm through the 2025 greenhouse tomato season. Preserved verbatim for quote mining and future reference.

Also see the two public Instagram posts:

---

Significant number of the cherry tomatoes wilted after planting. They perk back up overnight, but the ones that get blown on by the heater and a few during the day wilt. Water them in better next year. Some cherry tomatoes have open flowers at this point, and the slicers flowers are still curled up and tangled in the head.

Really sunny day, greenhouse overheated a bit and tomatoes were a little wilty. Enablingone roll up side.

Snow melt is at the point where puddles are everywhere outside. Western corner of the greenhouse has damp walkways, but no standing water. Surface of the beds still drying out. First cherry tomatoes, have set fruit.

Back half of the greenhouse is staying damp from snow melt and rain, but no standing water and the plants look happy. We've cut off irrigation to the rear half of the greenhouse. For some odd reason the first two tomato plants in the first row are the smallest and skinniest. Maybe shading from the wash pack. The tomatoes grew faster than the lettuce is this year and shaded them out. We may only get to harvest the southernmost lettuce.

The snow has pretty much finished melting, and the puddles are receding outside. Back half of the greenhouse is drying out we've reopened the hoses to that section. Finally have some sunshine! Bit of an aphid infestation on the peppers. They're not going after the tomatoes bad yet. Couple different beneficial insects on order. One tomato already has nine fruit set! But many tomatoes don't have any yet. Hot streak seems to have the most fruit set so far. Indigo has the most fruit set of the cherry tomatoes.

The lower leaves of second through fourth rows are getting pale so we started pruning three leaves per plant on those ones. A lot more fruit set over the last week. We have not established the routine of hitting the wires with the bamboo daily yet, we've done it a few times. We did come through and hand pollinated plants twice over the last week. Noticing a lot of flower clusters forming into a tomato sucker, unsure if this is a sign of anything. Some of the cherry tomatoes are now taller than me. Slicers are between chest and chin height.

Tomatoes in the areas that were wet have just as many fruit as tomatoes in other areas. Fruit clusters in general are more even than they were, not seeing a pattern. Eating our first cherry tomatoes this week, two weeks ahead of anticipated first harvest date. Probably from non-grafted tomatoes that we filled in gaps with. Seen the potentially signs of disease on the south facing side of the fourth row of tomatoes. Dry blackened, dead areas on the leaves. On the indigo cherry tomatoes, which are less disease resistant.

Landscape fabric laid down last week. Adjusted irrigation calculation for extra ground evaporation from pretending there were three lines to the actual four lines. We harvested a handful of pints of cherry tomatoes and a few slicer tomatoes. Still seeing a fair number of aborted flowers on the slicer tomatoes, but not on the cherry tomatoes. It was pretty cloudy for a lot of May, wondering if that's a factor. Tomatoes in the path of the heater airflow are ripening fruit first but also setting fewer fruits. There's a few plants in that section that are ripening the first fruits, but have only set fruit on their first 2 to 3 clusters and have been dropping flowers on the other clusters. We've been pruning leaves that have signs of gray mold and started pruning the bottom leaves of the cherries as they have over 18 leaves now. Added a photo of her current dehumidification program. I think I made some changes to it last year and I can't remember what the original settings were. Wondering if you guys have thoughts on that.

Our cherry tomatoes had somehow gotten up to 22 leaves per leader. So we pruned 5 to 6 leaves this week. Per your advice, we will get them back down to 14 leaves over the next two weeks. Slicer tomatoes, were finally getting up to or just over 18 leaves. Pruned two leaves per plant. We've been telling Orisha that there are fewer clusters set per leader and that the stems are still way too strong. Been super hot in there! I think the plants are starting to set fruit earlier. Wondering if our root stock is too vegetative steering, requiring a hotter temperature forcing.

It had been cloudy and smoky from Forest fires from Canada a lot over the past week. It was pretty wet in the greenhouse. Seen some signs of leaf mold, so we more aggressively pruned lower leaves and inner leaves. The southern most bed has a lot of fruit set, ranging from 18 to 26! Ruby Dawn's that have been ripening in the middle of the greenhouse have far fewer fruits, ranging from 4 to 12 after harvesting this week. You guys asked if there was a chance that we were having an ethylene problem with the heater, but it is power vented so I don't think so. We are using Orisha to run dehumidification cycles, but it is pretty wet and humid and here when it is wet outside. Supposedly our greenhouse plastic is treated to prevent condensation, but if it's lots of condensation and drips. Do cherry tomatoes pollinate more easily than slicers? Basically no aborted flowers on the cherries all season long.

"Tragedy has struck. We apparently had originally temporarily secured one of our trellis lines to the hoop house with zip ties. This hadn't been a problem over the past could of years, but this season the weight of the fruit was so great this week that a couple of zip ties broke and 5 tomato plants fell to the ground and snapped their stems. They had 29 pounds of fruit on them and would have potentially yielded around 100 lbs of fruit over the season.

Fruit seems to have not set at all on the slicers over the past week. It seems like every time we have a very cloudy week the plants refuse to set fruit. This time it seems like all of the slicers struggled to set fruit. The stem diameter is back to a good thickness, but the flower clusters were still producing suckers on the ends. Some warmer and sunnier days starting today, so we'll see if they start setting fruit again.

We had some minor fruit cracking last week, so I turned down the number of leaves in Orisha. Per Antoine's comment on plant exhaustion I turned up the number of clusters with set fruit.

I included a photo of the cherries that have not been pruned and lowered yet this week as well as the cherries that have."

"Unsure if it was moving the sensor or having some sunny days, but plants are setting fruit again within the top couple feet of the plant. Yields are starting to shoot up, we harvested 80 lbs of hot streaks from 48 bed feet in the last two days! We didn't harvest cherry tomatoes today, so the graphs don't look right for the weekly but we're four weeks ahead of production compared to last year despite the much colder spring. We did plant two weeks earlier this year.

I'm definitely considering building my next greenhouse to be 2 feet taller. Our cherry tomatoes and cucumbers reach the trellis lines before the bottom fruit have finished ripening so by the time we lower and lean we're then picking off the ground.

I just looked over our 2023 numbers. In the next week or two we will have harvested more pounds of cherry tomatoes than we did in all of 2023. And we will have done so before we even started harvesting cherry tomatoes that year!"

"Hot streaks by far have ripened the most fruit and are continuing to set flowers as well. Ruby dawn's are now ripening a lot of fruit and are continuing to set pretty well. Damsels ripened prematurely and did not set fruit well after that. They are going to have very low yields, probably for the rest of the summer. We will be making some varietal changes next year for sure. Similar story with the cherry tomatoes, the Sakura's are way out, producing the indigos and the sun golds. The best tomatoes among the hot streaks and the ruby dawns have a total of 12 flower clusters. Most of the damsels and some of the shorter plants have lagged behind that number.

I added fertilizer to the irrigation on what I thought was a slow trickle, but I've definitely been seeing an uptick in flower cluster suckers and sucker production on the cucumbers.

Next greenhouse we build, I'm going to make at least 2 feet taller. With the cucumbers and the cherry tomatoes, they reach the top line before the bottom fruit has finished ripening, and we end up setting fruits on the ground and have to harvest while kneeling. Over the winter, I might see if I can raise some of the trellising lines in the current greenhouse 2 feet"

"We hit 327 lbs of slicers this week! Last year's peak was 227 lbs. Our pounds per leader are not that much greater just 1.88 compared to 1.6. So, I'm attributing this mostly to optimized spacing and putting slicers in the southern most bed rather than cherries.

Last week plants were overly vegetative from the fertilizer boost, but they seem to be forming fewer suckers on flower clusters now. Fruit is setting, but still not uniformly. We have not been good about pollinating, but between pruning, harvesting, and the wind, I feel like they are getting shaken at least as much as hitting the wire with a stick. The folks we had pollinating with the vibrator missed a few shifts over the past weeks and we didn't work it into anybody else's schedule.

Starting to see the first signs of plant exhaustion in the form of smaller stems. I've set Orisha to 'weak.'

I've also increased the leaf count by two because I'm seeing a few plants with upcurled leaves and the soil seems a bit dry. Occasionally seeing some cracks on fruit near the stem, so I have mixed feelings about upping irrigation.

Cherry tomato and cucumber yields took a steep dive. Cherries from 140 lbs last week to 80 lbs this week. Cucumbers from 361 two weeks ago to 160 this week. Cucumbers peaked at only 187 lbs per week last year, so I'm not too concerned.

Last year we definitely went through cycles of getting behind on pruning and then needing to do a really hard prune. I thought the roller coast of yields was maybe related to that, but we have been staying on top of pruning and lowering and leaning."

Life has been busy on the farm! We had a record-breaking week of sales and our highest single market day of sales. Funny enough, this coincided with the downtick in our tomatoes, we've been harvesting a lot of salad, a lot of brassica, and a lot of peas. Tomatoes appear to be getting over their exhaustion from that first round of setting so much fruit. Seeing more fruit set along the plants. We adopted stick pollinating and trained the staff in on it so hopefully we start to see that more consistent fruit set. Plants are healthy and happy, just to touch two vigorous.
