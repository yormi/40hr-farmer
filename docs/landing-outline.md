* Starting with the bed that'll free you the most
    * Tomatoes sit on most market gardens already, and their ceiling is higher than any other crop you're growing. A well-run bed of greenhouse indeterminate tomatoes earns what 20 to 30 beds of beets, kale, or onions earn. That's the lever.
    * And tomatoes are the most labor-intensive beds on the farm. When yield doubles and your market is already buying every tomato you grow, you can cut half the beds and keep the same sales. Or keep the beds and automate a lot of the work, so the greenhouse stops demanding your attention around the clock. Either path, hours come back, and your most productive ground is yours again, to plant with something higher-margin.

* The techniques are known. Applying them from any farm's starting point is the open problem.
    * Big greenhouses have used these techniques for years. Our own Andrew Mefferd wrote [The Greenhouse and Hoophouse Grower's Handbook](https://www.johnnyseeds.com/tools-supplies/books/the-greenhouse-and-hoophouse-growers-handbook-andrew-mefferd-7683.html) on industrial greenhouse techniques, and we published the [6 Steps to $15,000](https://www.orisha.io/learn/6-steps-to-15000-inscription) course with Orisha last year.
    * Andrew applied them on his farm and doubled his yields. Quebec small growers working with consultants regularly hit 4x what the average grower does.
    * So the gap isn't the information. The gap is translation. Every farm starts from a different place: different climate, different history, different customers. Gordon's tomatoes last summer hit heat and bacterial spot, and we pruned him for fall vigor too late. That lesson went straight into the Playbook.
    * What's missing is making this translation easy for small growers. That's what this program is for.

* The Program
    * Built around the translation problem. Each month, one goal small growers can act on in their own greenhouse, from their own starting point.
    * Focus: greenhouse. This year: tomatoes. (Cucumbers, then lettuce after.)
    * What a week looks like: 2 to 3 hours. Monthly goal, short videos, live coaching. Mostly work you were already going to do.
    * First goal: the main reason a fruit doesn't sell is it didn't set in the first place. Everyone we've seen, even Drew, is at half the fruit load a plant can carry. That's the biggest gain available, so it's the first goal: build fruit load up without exhausting plants.
    * The Playbook is a living thing. It grows every season from what farmers surface in their own fields, and the tools get better as we go.
