## Piece 1: "Where we are" section

A self-contained origin story that sits between the hero and the `#problem` section and resets the voice of the whole page.

### Title, three options

1. Where we are (primary)
2. How we got here
3. The playbook is not finished yet

Guillaume's call. Option 1 is the quietest, most honest frame. Option 2 is more narrative. Option 3 is the most transparent about the stage we are in, and probably the most disarming.

### Draft copy (primary version)

> **Where we are**
>
> In 2024, Drew at Ghost House Farm installed Orisha to run the climate and irrigation in his greenhouse. By the end of that season he had harvested around 2,000 pounds of tomatoes. Roughly 250% of his 2023 yield. Doubled.
>
> The moment that reframed everything for us came later, when Drew mentioned that his wife Allison had quit her job to work full-time on the farm. That was the real story. The yield was a means. The life behind the yield was the point.
>
> Every farmer we talk to loves what they do, and is quietly crushed by the pressure of it. Too much to do, all at once. So we stopped thinking about yield for its own sake and started thinking about pressure relief through yield. Getting paid enough that you can actually live the life you chose. Greenhouse tomatoes turned out to be the best place to start: highest revenue per bed, by a wide margin. One tomato bed can do the work of twenty or thirty beds of beets or peas.
>
> In 2025 we started consulting. Drew was our first remote client. Gordon at Ten Mothers Farm joined in late spring. Within a week of working together, Gordon wrote to us: *"Pollination has definitely improved, the tapping works. We've doubled fruit load in 2 weeks."* Then heat and bacterial spot erased the win. We hadn't yet learned that he pauses sales in August, so we could have pruned fruit to carry the plants strong into fall. That lesson went into the playbook.
>
> Drew's 2025 season started rough. Wilted seedlings, a month behind in May. We fixed pollination in week two and fruit load went up four times in fourteen days. By July 1, he had beaten his entire 2023 season, before the point on the calendar when he had even started harvesting cherry tomatoes in 2023.
>
> *"I just looked over our 2023 numbers. In the next week or two we will have harvested more pounds of cherry tomatoes than we did in all of 2023. And we will have done so before we even started harvesting cherry tomatoes that year."* Drew, mid-July 2025.
>
> Even this season, with a 327 pound single-week record, Drew carried about half the fruit load a plant can actually support. Our best current result is half of what is possible.
>
> Cohort 1 opens in May/June 2026. The first monthly goal is straightforward: maximize fruit load without exhausting the plant. The playbook is not finished. We are building it with this cohort, carefully, with a small group, because going too fast would break what we are trying to build.

Word count on the prose, excluding quotes: roughly 260.

### Alternates for the key lines

**Alternate opening (if the primary feels too dense):**

> In 2024, Drew at Ghost House Farm installed Orisha in his greenhouse. By the end of the season, he had harvested around 2,000 pounds of tomatoes. Double his 2023 yield.

**Alternate thesis line (instead of "pressure relief through yield"):**

> So we stopped optimizing for yield alone and started optimizing for the life behind it. Enough income, in enough time, to actually be present for the people and reasons you started farming for.

**Alternate closing (if Guillaume wants a softer invitation):**

> Cohort 1 opens in May/June 2026. We are keeping it small on purpose. The playbook is not finished, and this cohort is where we finish writing it together.

### Pull quote handling

I put Drew's 2023 comparison quote inline in the body because it carries the arc. Gordon's quote stays inline too, framed as the partial win that became a lesson. If the visual design wants a large callout pull quote, the Drew line is the stronger candidate.

### Notes on voice and trade-offs

- Structure follows the beats, weighted toward Drew because his story is the spine. Gordon is a single paragraph because the "partial win turned into playbook lesson" beat is the point, not a second full origin.
- I chose "pressure relief through yield" as the thesis line because it names the emotional insight without making a claim. Open to simpler.
- I kept "half of what is possible" at the end of Drew's 2025 beat because it is the most honest thing we can say: the best current result still has headroom, and we are transparent about that. It also earns the "the playbook is not finished" line that closes the section.
- Allison's story is compressed to one sentence plus the thesis shift, rather than a full scene. The existing landing page already has Drew's pull quote about his wife quitting her job, and I did not want to repeat it here and dilute it. This section sets up that quote rather than restating it.
- No claims. No "you will." The section shows what happened and what we learned. The reader draws the conclusion.

## Piece 2: Hero sub-headline, light touch

- Alternate 1 is the most humble, explicitly invitational. Alternate 2 leads with the question, which matches the "learning loop" positioning in `docs/brand/purpose.md`. Primary is tightest.

>

Word count: 69.

### Notes on voice and trade-offs

- Kept the existing H2 *"Farming is beautiful. Let's make sure it stays that way."* because it bookends the hero and it works. The brief said replace the FOMO line, not the whole heading.
- Removed the number 50. Even stating the cap reads as a scarcity lever. The capacity frame is "small on purpose for the work we are doing," not "50 is the cap, act fast."
- "Going faster would break what we are trying to build" is the honest, humble line doing the heaviest lift. It names ambition (build something bigger) and restraint (not yet) in the same breath.
- "If greenhouse tomatoes are in your season" keeps the self-selection gate from `docs/brand/ideal-farmer.md` without feeling like a filter.
- "We'd love to hear from you" is the softest opt-in we can write without going limp. Open to swap for something more neutral if Guillaume prefers.
- The existing form copy below (*"No spam. Just updates on the launch and early access."*) can stay. It does not conflict.

## Decisions Guillaume may want to push back on

- **I cut the number "50"** from the final CTA entirely. The brief said drop FOMO, and any specific cap number tends to read as scarcity even when framed gently. If Guillaume wants the number back for concreteness, I'd reinsert it in the "Where we are" section as context rather than in the CTA.
- **I used "around 2,000 pounds" and "roughly 250%"** rather than exact figures. Drew's raw notes don't give a single clean 2024 total, and the brief's numbers were approximate. If Guillaume has the exact 2024 total, swap it in and drop "around."
- **I kept Gordon as a short beat, not a parallel origin.** His story as given is a partial win that turned into a playbook lesson. Stretching it to match Drew's weight would either require more facts than we have or risk overclaiming. If Guillaume wants more Gordon, I'd need one or two more concrete details from the 2025 season with him.
- **I moved "cut labor without cutting sales"** out of the hero sub-headline. It still lives in the page `<title>` meta tag and in the nav/OG description. If Guillaume wants it kept on-page, easiest is to keep the existing descriptor line and add the new sub-headline as a fourth line instead of replacing.
- **I interpreted "sub-headline" as the descriptor line** (third line in the hero), not the poetic second line *"But life is richer when you still have energy for the people in it."* That line is the best sentence on the page and I didn't want to touch it. Confirm that reading.
- **No dashes check:** I reread every line above. All pauses are commas, periods, colons, or rewrites. The one hyphen that appears is in compound words inside source-material quotes I was told to preserve (none found in the quotes I used), and in "full-time," "self-contained," "sub-headline," which are compound modifiers, not stylistic punctuation. If Guillaume reads "no dashes" as "no hyphens anywhere," I can rewrite those compounds.
